Cooperation for fun and (not-for) profit

Introduction

Many people hear cooperative and think of different things. Some think of radical hippie and/or religious communes; some think of REI. Cooperation is more a set of principles than a specific implementation or use and this is a good thing.

Worker cooperatives exist to protect the rights of the employees of a business by allowing the employees to democratically make decisions about the board, what to do with profits, and company policy. Consumer cooperatives exist to allow consumers to have economic strength in numbers.

Housing cooperatives are a different class of thing, because they are not-for-profit. The goal of a cooperative housing organization is affordable rent for tenants and democratic control for the tenants over the finances and activities of the organization.

See a pattern here?

I live in the great state of Texas, and so the following guide is most applicable there. However, there are similar statutes and provisions in other states which allow for similar schemes.

I say "scheme" because what I am about to propose is counter to the way of life with which most people are accustomed. I'm talking about a mutually respectful, labor-centered existence; an existence where a person's value is not measured in dollars, and where Capital has been dethroned; a life post-rat-race.

So, let's get this party started.

Who the Hell are you?

I'm Gatlin Johnson; this is my website. I currently reside in cooperative housing and have been on the Board of Directors of College Houses, Inc. Currently, I coordinate the labor at a 100+ person house in Austin, TX.

I am a big fan of cooperatives and I really believe that the cooperative movement provides solutions to a lot of problems we face socially and politically. If the banks are screwing you, switch to a credit union. If you're sick of your land-lord, become your own land-lord. Want better food at your super market? Join a food co-op and vote.

The list goes on. Group equity housing, as will be described in this document, is but one way of structuring a housing cooperative. I think it is the best model to empower the disenfranchised and poor because expansion only lowers rent.

Hopefully this guide will be useful for you. If you have any questions, comments, or concerns, please send me a letter at rokenrol+coop@gmail.com.

What is a co-op?

In general, a co-op is owned by its members. Aside from any other details, that is the most important quality. We can get a little more specific, though.

Rochdale

There is a set of 7 principles, called the Rochdale Principles, which are generally agreed upon to be the guidelines to which cooperatives should aspire:

  1. Voluntary and open membership. Cooperatives are open to anyone who wants to join, full stop.

  2. Democratic member control. Members are active in setting policy and making decisions as a group. It is up to the group itself to decide exactly how this should work; some groups use simple majority, some are consensus, and still others operate differently.

  3. Member economic participation. Members contribute equitably (perhaps proportionately according to some standard or absolutely equally) and decide democratically what to do with funds.

  4. Autonomy and independence. At all costs, in any relationship with any governmental or non-governmental entity, the organization maintains democratic member control and never cedes this control for any reason.

  5. Education, training, and information. The cooperative should provide education and training for their members so that everyone can contribute effectively. The organization is a human one; it should help humans grow and learn.

  6. Cooperation among cooperatives. Strength in numbers!

  7. Concern for community. No man is an island; a cooperative organization should be concerned for the welfare of its community, however broadly or narrowly defined.

You may read more about the Rochdale Principles here. In general, though, this summary gets the gist across. It is important to remember that nothing legally obliges you to follow these rules but (anecdotally) they do result in functioning houses.

Group equity

The specific kind of cooperative I will be writing about is a group-equity organization.

In a group-equity scenario, the financial value of the organization stays in the organization and is not paid back out to members. Instead, the spirit of the organization is to take the value it accumulates and aggressively try to lower rent, provide more services, and generally work even harder in the interests of the members. Democratic member control ensures that this happens.

Other kinds of cooperatives are more familiar: the amount given to the cooperative is paid back (with some kind of added amount) to the member if the member chooses to leave; or, dividends may be paid directly to the member. This is attractive to any self-interested entity for obvious reasons. The problem, though, is that this does not poise the organization for growth.

Another problem with other models is liability: what happens when someone gets hurt on property directly owned by you? You are potentially opened up to lawsuits and insurance nightmares. This sounds awful.

Group equity, then, maintains a strong financial core within the organization, and all shared property is technically owned by the organization. This provides protection from liability and makes the organization easier to maintain. The downside is that investments are not paid back directly, but through services.

The win with group equity is that the organization benefits from expansion. But we are getting ahead of ourselves here. Additionally, shared bank accounts and bulk purchasing are easier when only one entity (legally) has control.

Democratic control

This sounds like a nightmare, of course. If you can barely schedule a lunch with 4 friends, how can you run a large housing group?

The first step, of course, is not to be a large housing group. College Houses, Inc of Austin, Texas (the cooperative with which the author is best-acquainted) has 6 different houses. 5 of them have 100+ or more members. In general, this number proves to be effective; the author would discourage a house from aspiring to any more. Most other houses this author has encountered have around 20 members. Many have fewer.

The organization itself is rather huge but, just as with federal and state governments, at a certain threshold new layers of government are added for efficiency. Yes, I said "government" and "efficiency" in the same sentence.

Houses tend to elect leadership positions charged with varying amounts of responsibility. The spirit is not to endow people with power over others, but to serve the rest of the house. Many houses even call the keystone leader "Trustee." ("Director" is also common but that sounds so depressing, doesn't it?).

Depending on the circumstances of the house, there are a number of options available, which will be discussed. Houses tend to have labor managers, maintenance coordinators, groundskeepers, chefs, and many other positions chosen straight from the membership itself. Depending on how many people are in your individual house, you can create more or fewer positions.

Meetings

Houses also tend to hold meetings twice or four times a month. House policy is typically discussed, created, amended, or redacted at these meetings. Additionally, these meetings serve as a general forum for issues facing the house.

It is customary to require any proposed changes to house policy to be posted a day or two before the meeting; this is up to the house to decide in its bylaws.

Hierarchy

Many cooperatives seem to frown on hierarchy, and in the case of a housing co-op it really has no place.

However, sometimes (for example) a maintenance coordinator will have a maintenance crew and the coordinator will hand out assignments to the crew. This is an administrative convenience. While a great number of atrocities have been committed in the spirit of efficiency and consolidation, many houses permit any member to have an officer reviewed for any reason. Thus, everyone has a check on everyone else. If the house is laid back and fun then members should rarely have to resort to this kind of action.

Labor

Cooperatives are said to be more "laborist" than "capitalist." While a capital investment is generally required in order to make the house function, the primary goal of a housing cooperative is to make this capital investment as low as possible.

Houses tend to have labor requirements, such as washing dishes or cooking or mopping or any number of other menial or administrative tasks. This is the true power of cooperation: the members rely on each other to run the house as opposed to paying external entities. This strengthens the financial position of the house and allows the members to keep costs down. It also inspires a camraderie among members which is hard to quantify.

If you are skeptical of this, consider that College Houses has 532 members at the time of writing. The organization is managed at an adminstrative level by 5 paid staff. Roughly 19% of organizational revenue goes to the staff.

Officers

There is a fine line to be walked between a completely flat, honor-based system of operations and a rigid authoritarian dystopia. Many houses create officers whose stature may be challenged at any time.

A house typically has the following positions. Note that these are only suggestions but they do work well.

  • Trustee. The Trustee has the vaguest but arguably most important job: ensuring that the co-op is healthy and operational. The Trustee oversees other officers in their duties, helps mediate disputes among members, and tries to improve the house.

  • Labor Coordinator. Many houses choose the cheeky alternative "Labor Czar," and yours truly likes it more. The Labor Czar coordinates what labor assignments need to be done, appropriate compensation, and who will be doing it. They do not have control per se but typically they are empowered to make reasonable and fair executive decisions about who will be doing what.

  • Kitchen Manager. A co-op typically has a very large kitchen, and a large store of foods. Many times the kitchen has industrial sinks, grills, sanitizers, and similar equipment because technically the kitchen has to be up to local health codes. The Kitchen Manager ensures that the kitchen is stocked, equipped, clean, and functional. No small feat.

  • Maintenance Coordinator. Get someone who knows how to fix the majority of things that can go wrong in a house, and who doesn't mind being dirty, and elect them to this position.

  • Treasurer. We manage our finances in a college-ruled notebook somewhere. This doesn't have to be fancy.

Conclusion

The emphasis thus far has been to explain the more quantifiable and objective properties of co-ops. It is one thing to say that co-ops are fun and bring a real sense of community; it is another to demonstrate a financial model which scales well and provides useful services to its members.

How do I start one?

Cooperatives are very tricky. The first step, of course, is to have a community. This might be the single hardest thing to do simply because many people have to need housing at the same time for it to work. This is why cooperatives have been so successful among students: you have a predictable cycle of people coming and going.

Once you have a community, though, it gets a little easier. The Texas Business Organizations Code, Title 6 Chapter 251 Outlines the legal basis for creating "cooperative associations." Let us take a look at what a cooperative association is.

Cooperative basis and the legal foundations

It is best to go ahead and become an official cooperative association legally, for a number of reasons. Chief among them include liability shielding and simplified finances.

Additionally, this puts you in a good place to go to an organization like NASCO or a credit union and request assistance - both legally and financially.

According to the official definitions, a cooperative association may accumulate savings and retain them at the organizational level to reduce charges to patrons, expand services, or just act in accordance with the organizational charter.

This is what is known as a "cooperative basis." Dividends may be paid to each individual member or to the organization itself, which means group equity is completely legal.

Additionally, provisions from the code governing non-profits also apply to cooperative associations. However, the rules of cooperative associations explicitly trump the provisions for non-profits. So, we will only delve into non-profit rules if we strictly have to here.

To start a cooperative association, the following information must be filed with the Secretary of State:

  • The name of the entity being formed;
  • The type of filing entity being formed;
  • The purpose for which the filing entity is formed;
  • The duration (you should just say "perpetually");
  • The street address of the initial registered office and the name of the person at the office;
  • The name and adress of each filing entity;
  • Whether the coop is organized with or without shares (that's "no") here;
  • The number of shares or memberships (irrelevant);
  • The method of distribution on winding up and termination of any surplus of the cooperative association; and
  • The names and street addresses of the directors who will manage the affairs of the coop for the initial year.

That's quite a large pill to swallow. Even worse, the Secretary of State website does not provide a form for this. However, the form for non-profits can serve as inspiration for whatever document you create.

It'll only cost $25, though, if that makes you feel better.

Now we will go over some of the nit-picky details of cooperative associations and the weird sorts of things you have to do.

Articles of Incorporation and Bylaws

The Articles of Incorporation are the forms you file with the state to form the cooperative. They specify the information detailed in the previous section and really whatever else you want. However, changing Articles of Incorporation can be difficult and expensive because it requires a vote and a filing fee. What you should do instead is mention in your articles that day-to-day operations are specified in bylaws maintained by the Board of Directors.

And that's where the Bylaws come in.

The Bylaws need to specify everything about how your organization is run. What positions are there on the board? How long do they serve? How do you handle new member applications? What rights do members have? What constitutes a "majority" in a vote? What subdivisions of the organization are there?

The list can go on endlessly, but in practice efficient sets of bylaws are possible. As is usual with this document, I refer you to the College Houses, Inc, bylaws.

Let's take a look at some of what they do.

  • Purpose This is very important for legal reasons. It also serves to focus the organization. Never underestimate the importance of a mission statement.

  • Membership meetings Who, what, where, when, and how. The "why" is implicit.

  • Board of Directors This is a really huge part. Notice the detail and especially notice how they plan for the worst (ie, directors leaving, ousting them, etc).

  • Positions How the Board itself actually functions.

  • Employees Not everyone in the corporation is a member, and it's good to specify when and how salaried employees are managed. Let it be known, though, that you open yourself up to a number of legal headaches when doing this. Best skip it for now.

  • How committees work In practice, the most important three committees are three that are not specified in the bylaws. However, Article IX, Section 1 allows for them.

  • Money stuff When does the fiscal year start? When can the corporation hoard money? When can the corporation spend money? Notice the lack of officious language, too.

  • Amendments Possibly the most important part of the bylaws: the rules by which the bylaws themselves are amended. Don't skimp on this one.

I think the most important take-away is how clean and concise the language is. This is not legal weasel-ese, it is language by humans and for humans. So, don't feel like you must affect some odd stuffy Victorian writing style.

Board of Directors

You must have a Board of Directors because technically you are a legal entity. This may not be obvious simply because you wanted to buy a house, not create some kind of business with a board and all that crap. Fret not!

The legal requirements for a board are as follows:

  • There must be 5 directors;
  • No director may serve more than 3 continuous years;
  • Directors are directors until expunged (as provided by the bylaws) or replaced in an election;
  • The number of directors may be divvied among arbitrary parts of the organization;
  • An executive committee may be elected if specified in the bylaws.

The Board is technically the actual legal steward of the cooperative, even if that's not in the spirit of your house. That's fine; deprive them of whatever powers you don't want them to have in your bylaws.

Once elected (or, right after the cooperative is formed), the Board must elect officers. The default officers provided by law are:

  • A president;
  • A secretary and a treasurer or a secretary-treasurer;
  • one or more vice-presidents.

You can, however, make up whatever you want. Several organizations have Chair of the Board in place of President. College Houses has a Chair, a Secretary, and a Treasurer.

Note also that the Board does not have to consist solely of members of the organization. Again citing College Houses, you can have special positions for interested community members.

What I suggest you do if you only have one house in your cooperative is to make elected leadership positions in your house automatically be board members. Houses typically have multiple officer positions anyway and this keeps things relatively simple.

If you have multiple houses, it might be best to actually give the board some power. At two or more houses, the board is useful for inter-house coordination. There will be budget concerns applicable to the whole organization, purchasing decisions which affect all the members, and maybe even shared coffers which individual houses might want to dip into.

The take away: if it's just you and a few buddies, write the board into your bylaws as a ceremonial legal necessity. If you start expanding, make use of the board since you have to have it anyway.
